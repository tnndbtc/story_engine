Before you research, generate 5 candidate opening hooks for this topic.
Each hook is 1–2 sentences (what the viewer hears in the first 5 seconds).

Score each on three criteria (1–5 each):
  - Curiosity gap: does it withhold the key fact and make the viewer need to know?
  - Stakes: does it make the viewer feel something is at risk for them or people they care about?
  - Clarity: does the viewer know WHY they should keep watching in under 3 seconds?

Also assign each hook a type from this list (pick the single best fit):
  mystery          — withholds a key fact the viewer must watch to discover
  hidden_mechanism — reveals that something works differently than everyone assumes
  reversal         — expected outcome flipped; thing succeeded/failed for opposite reason
  collapse         — system, person, or plan that should have worked just broke
  irony            — entity does the exact thing it was designed to prevent
  betrayal         — trusted party acted against the people who depended on them

Select the hook with the highest total score. Record its type as hook_type.
This becomes your narrative anchor. Proceed to research with that hook in mind —
your goal is to find the facts that prove and deepen it, not to find a different story.

Also generate thumbnail_text: 3–6 words MAX, punchy, for the YouTube thumbnail image.
NOT the title. The visual hook that makes someone stop scrolling.

RULE 1 — SUBJECT ANCHOR (mandatory): thumbnail_text MUST name the main subject —
  the company, person, technology, place, or asset the story is about.
  A viewer who has never heard this story must be able to identify the TOPIC from
  thumbnail_text alone. If they can't, rewrite it.

RULE 2 — FORMAT: [Subject] + [Drama]. Both are required.
  The subject anchors; the drama creates urgency or curiosity.

GOOD examples (subject + drama):
  "Bitcoin: No Plan for 2140"     ← subject=Bitcoin, drama=no plan
  "SpaceX Spent $60B on Code"     ← subject=SpaceX, drama=absurd spend
  "Two Trains, One Track"         ← subject=trains, drama=collision setup
  "Robinhood Fires 290"           ← subject=Robinhood, drama=mass layoff
  "SEC Blocked Every Path"        ← subject=SEC, drama=total block

BAD examples (drama only — subject missing):
  "21 Million. Then What?"        ← what is this about? Bitcoin? People? Dollars?
  "Safety Gap Kills"              ← kills what? trains? planes? systems?
  "$21B They Don't Have"          ← who? for what?
  "Fired for Success?"            ← who got fired?
  "Top 20. Twice."                ← top 20 what? who?

YOUTUBE ARC (internal structure — do NOT label these):
  0–5s   (opening sentence):   Hook — the most surprising or consequential fact,
                                delivered without context. No "Today we're looking at."
                                No "In this video." Start mid-story.
  5–20s  (next 2–3 sentences): Stakes — why this matters to THIS viewer, specifically.
                                Make it personal or make the scale feel real.
  20–60s (next 4–6 sentences): Escalation — the mechanism. What is actually driving
                                this? What happened step by step? This is the real story.
  60s+   (remaining body):     Depth + Consequence — what does this mean for real people?
                                A specific person, company, or country that is changed by this.
                                Concrete numbers. One vivid detail that makes it stick.
  End:   A sharp insight or a deliberate open loop — a question that makes the NEXT
         video feel necessary. NOT "subscribe for more." Something that sticks.

RETENTION RULE: Plant one unanswered question every 15–25 seconds of narration.
Never fully explain the most interesting thing before the midpoint.

LENGTH: 180–220 words (STRICT)
  - Count words in your draft before finalizing
  - If too long → compress ruthlessly; cut any sentence that restates what was already said
  - If under 180 → go DEEPER into what you already found:
      • Show what a specific affected person actually experiences
      • Develop the mechanism one level further — what happens step by step
      • Add the concrete consequence — what changes for real people after this
      • Describe the scale — how many people, how much money, how wide the impact
      Do NOT introduce new topics. Do NOT invent details not in your searches.

TITLE FORMULA: [Specific Actor/Entity] + [Counterintuitive Result] + [Implied Gap]
  RULE: titles must NOT summarize. They must CREATE a gap — something the viewer
  needs to close by watching.

  The strongest pattern from our highest-retention videos:
    Specific named actor or object + concrete counterintuitive action/result → viewer must watch to understand HOW

  Proven high-retention examples (real titles, 30–70% avg view percentage):
    "Why blaming the pilots just stopped working in court"           ← mechanism inversion
    "One GitHub Employee Installed This Extension. 3,800 Repos Were Gone."  ← scale from one person
    "India just broke a fuel price freeze nobody admits existed"    ← hidden fact revealed
    "One word on a coffee cup just ended a CEO's career in Korea"   ← absurdly specific cause → huge effect
    "A bird-watching trip just turned a cruise into a floating outbreak"  ← weird-but-real mechanism

  Good (create gap):
    Specific actor or object + result that seems impossible → "how?"
    Hidden fact about a named institution the viewer didn't know → "why?"
    Ordinary trigger → disproportionate consequence → "wait, how?"

  Bad (summarize):
    "OpenAI releases new model"         ← no gap, understood without watching
    "Federal Reserve raises rates"      ← pure announcement, no mechanism
    "Company reports record profits"    ← fact, not curiosity
    "AI is changing jobs"               ← vague, no specific actor or result
    "You might want to know this..."    ← filler with no concrete hook

  FORBIDDEN title patterns:
    - Opening with "You" or "Your" (viewer doesn't feel addressed, they feel sold to)
    - Vague entity + vague verb: "Tech Giant Announces New Policy"
    - Pure price/score/number updates: "Bitcoin hits $X" or "Stock falls Y%"
    - Claim/deny political statements: "Country X says Country Y did Z"
    - Generic "AI" as actor with no specific company, product, or person:
        BAD:  "Every AI system has a blind spot only humans can see"    ← no named entity
        BAD:  "The AI model you're using helped hackers break security" ← vague + "you're"
        BAD:  "AI is quietly changing how banks work"                   ← no actor, no gap
        GOOD: "One GitHub Employee Installed This Extension. 3,800 Repos Were Gone."
        GOOD: "The Protocol War That Decided AI's Future Happened in Four Months"
        RULE: if the story is about AI, name the specific company, model, or person.
              "AI" alone is never a specific enough actor.

  If your title can be understood without watching the video → rewrite it.

WRITING RULES:
  0. NO BACKGROUND BEFORE CONFLICT — the first sentence MUST be conflict, tension, or
     a surprising fact. NEVER open with context, history, or "for years..." setup.
     If your draft opens with background → delete it and start from the conflict.
  1. SINGLE STORYLINE — one main causal chain; no parallel explanations
  2. EVERY SENTENCE PUSHES FORWARD — if it does not advance the story, cut it
  3. SELECTIVE FACTS — use only the most important facts; combine multiple facts
     into one idea when possible; do NOT try to include everything you found
  4. NO STRUCTURAL ARTIFACTS — no bullets, no "First... Second... Finally...",
     no labeled sections, no report-style writing
  5. NATURAL SPOKEN ENGLISH — short to medium sentences, direct, conversational;
     write exactly how a smart person would explain this to a friend on a walk;
     no jargon, no academic tone, no filler phrases like "it's worth noting"
  6. COMPRESSION OVER COMPLETENESS — clarity and momentum, not exhaustive coverage

WORLD / POLITICS STORY FRAMING — apply this whenever your story involves
governments, military, diplomacy, or geopolitical actors:

  WEAK (produces low retention — avoid this structure):
    Scenario: "Iran claimed responsibility for striking a US vessel.
    The Pentagon denied the strike caused significant damage.
    Officials said tensions remain high."
    → Pure statement war. No mechanism revealed. Viewer already knew tensions existed.

  STRONG (reveals mechanism — do this instead):
    Scenario: "The weapon Iran used was designed specifically to defeat the
    Phalanx close-in defense system. The USS Lincoln had the system installed
    but the countermeasure protocol wasn't activated — because of a 2019
    maintenance policy change that nobody reversed."
    → Reveals institutional failure. Viewer understands something new.

  WEAK: "Israeli settlers attacked Palestinian villages amid rising tensions."
    → Vague conflict summary. Viewer already knew this was happening.

  STRONG: "A 1967 military decree gives settler militias authority that
    exceeds the Israeli army's chain of command — and it has never been
    legally challenged because of a single procedural ambiguity in the
    original text."
    → Hidden institutional mechanism. Viewer learns the actual structure.

  THE TEST: after your opening paragraph, ask yourself:
    "Has the viewer learned HOW something works, or just THAT something happened?"
    If the answer is "just that something happened" → rewrite to reveal the mechanism.

FORBIDDEN:
  - Do NOT mention source names in the narrative text itself
  - Do NOT invent facts not found in your searches
  - Do NOT generalize beyond what you found
  - Do NOT use "You might think... but actually..." more than once
  - Do NOT open with a question ("Have you ever wondered...")

QUALITY CHECK (mandatory before finalizing body):
  - Count the words in body. Target 180–220. If not there → expand or compress.
    Always output body with text — never leave body as "".
  - Can the entire story be summarized in ONE sentence? If not → rewrite
  - Is there exactly ONE main idea? If not → cut
  - Does any sentence feel optional? → remove it
  - Does the title create a gap (not summarize)?
  - Does the ending create a strong, memorable takeaway?
  - Did you invent any fact not found in your searches? → remove it
