SCORING GUIDE:

curiosity_gap (0-20)
  High: the premise is counterintuitive or impossible-sounding — creates
        immediate "wait, what?" before the viewer understands the context.
    "A virus that shouldn't exist showed up on a cruise ship"
    "A 732-byte script broke every Linux system since 2017"
    "The Fed's inflation model has a 40-year-old flaw nobody fixed"
  Low: states an obvious or expected outcome.
    "Government announces new policy"  "Company reports quarterly earnings"

ordinary_people_stakes (0-20)
  Ask ONE question: is the VIEWER'S OWN device, money, account, health, job,
  or daily workflow already being affected — right now, without them knowing?

  High (15–20): the viewer IS the person affected. Their specific device, bank
        account, workplace tool, or body is implicated directly.
    "Google quietly put 4GB on YOUR computer" → YOUR device, already done
    "The bank race rewriting how YOUR money moves" → YOUR money, ongoing now
    "AI consuming the chips YOUR phone was supposed to get" → YOUR hardware
    "This virus mutation could reach YOUR city" → YOUR health risk

  Medium (7–14): affects the viewer's industry, country, or financial system
        in a tangible way — they need to infer their personal connection.
    "The Fed's model has a flaw affecting all savings" → requires inference
    "A supply chain shift changing tech prices globally" → indirect impact

  Low (0–6): affects OTHER specific people, companies, or countries.
        The viewer watches from the outside with no direct personal stake.
    "CEO signed 4,000 offer letters then cut 1,100 jobs" → other people's jobs
    "Brazil's infrastructure paid out $400" → another country's problem
    "Iran's football team can't enter the host country" → other people's sport
    "Coinbase posted record market share and a $394M loss" → a company's results
    "Two nations dispute territorial rights" → distant geopolitics

  CRITICAL: a story that affects "people in general" or "society broadly" is NOT
  high stakes for the viewer. The viewer must be the specific person affected.
  Famous institutions and large numbers do NOT raise this score.

hidden_mechanism (0-20)
  High: reveals HOW something works that most people assumed was different,
        didn't know existed, or never thought to question.
    "The reason your flight keeps getting delayed is actually tied to
     a 1970s maintenance contract loophole"
    "BYD's sales collapse at home is funding its overseas expansion"
  Low: event recap with no mechanism — just what happened, not why or how.
    "Country X condemned Country Y's statement"
    "Bitcoin rose 8% after market open"
  IMPORTANT: the mechanism must be non-obvious. Reporting that "bad management
  caused the failure" is NOT a hidden mechanism.

consequence_clarity (0-15)
  High: viewer can state in one sentence what changes for them or for the
        world after watching. The cause-effect chain is explicit and crisp.
    "After watching, I understand why my streaming library keeps shrinking"
  Medium: consequence is real but requires inference.
  Low: the story ends without a clear "so what" — viewer learns facts
       but cannot describe the consequence.

audience_fit (0-15)
  Directly score how well this story matches the English-speaking channel audience above.
  High (12-15): story IS the channel — hidden system, quiet shift, weird-but-real,
                ordinary stakes relevant to English-speaking audiences globally.
                Audience would recommend it.
    Examples of 15: "The forensic detail that proved friendly fire didn't happen"
                    "AI is replacing the chip supply chain silently"
                    "Why the US banking system lets fraudsters drain accounts in 10 seconds"
  Medium (6-11): fits some channel traits but has elements the audience dislikes,
                 OR is a strong story geographically narrow to Asia without
                 clear English-speaker relevance.
  Low (0-5): story is primarily geopolitical posturing, sports, price recap,
             or requires heavy prior context. Includes stories about Asian
             regional politics or culture with no clear English-speaker impact.
    Examples of 0-3: "Iran claims it struck a US warship / Pentagon denies"
                     "Bitcoin price breaks $81k — analysts watch key levels"
                     "Orlando fires coach after playoff exit"
                     "Taiwan's legislature debates procedural rules again"
  EXCEPTION — do NOT penalize for geopolitics if the story's core is a hidden
  mechanism, forensic finding, or institutional failure:
    "A buckshot pellet traced to a specific gun ended the friendly-fire debate"
      → high hidden_mechanism + high audience_fit (it's a forensics story)
    "A North Korean hacker unit exploited a bank's legacy SWIFT code"
      → high hidden_mechanism (it's a systems story, not a diplomatic story)
    "China's chip export controls created an invisible supply chain nobody mapped"
      → mechanism is global impact on English-speaking tech industry
  The test: could the story run with the political actors anonymised and still
  be compelling to an English-speaking viewer? If yes, score audience_fit on
  the mechanism, not the politics.

title_retention_alignment (0-5)
  Does the title WITHHOLD the payoff so the viewer MUST watch to resolve it?
  This is NOT about accuracy — it is about whether the title creates an open
  question that cannot be answered without watching.

  High (4–5): title raises a strong question the viewer cannot answer from
        the title alone. The punchline, mechanism, or outcome is hidden.
    "The Number Tech CEOs Are Racing to Brag About" → what number? must watch
    "Spent His Final Years Doing This" → doing WHAT? must watch
    "Chrome quietly put something on your computer" → WHAT? must watch
    "A bird-watching trip turned a cruise into a floating outbreak" → HOW?

  Medium (2–3): title hints at something surprising but partially reveals it.

  Low (0–1): title states the entire story. The viewer already knows the
        punchline after reading the title. Nothing left to discover.
    "This CEO signed 4,000 offer letters then cut 1,100 jobs for AI"
        → irony is fully stated, nothing to resolve, no reason to watch
    "This AI just wrote 100% of a developer's code for a month"
        → conclusion is in the title, story is already told
    "Coinbase posted record market share and a $394M loss"
        → both facts stated, no open question remains
    "This $400 Payout Reveals Brazil's Hidden Infrastructure Problem"
        → "reveals" is in the title, tension pre-resolved

low_context_accessibility (0-5)
  High: someone with zero domain expertise understands the core story
        within the first 30 seconds. No jargon assumed.
  Low: requires specialist background to follow without explanation.
